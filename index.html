Legendary Astronomers & Astrophysicists

Galileo Galilei (1564–1642, Italy)

Father of modern observational astronomy; discovered Jupiter's moons, Venus phases, and sunspots using his telescope.

Johannes Kepler (1571–1630, Germany)

Formulated the three laws of planetary motion, showing orbits are elliptical.

Isaac Newton (1643–1727, England)

Developed universal gravitation and laws of motion, foundational to celestial mechanics.

Edwin Hubble (1889–1953, USA)

Proved the universe is expanding and galaxies exist beyond the Milky Way.

Carl Sagan (1934–1996, USA)

Popularized science through Cosmos; pioneered exobiology and planetary studies.

Stephen Hawking (1942–2018, UK)

Revolutionized black hole physics and cosmology with Hawking radiation theory.

Neil deGrasse Tyson (1958–, USA)

Modern science communicator; director of Hayden Planetarium and astrophysicist.

Vera Rubin (1928–2016, USA)

Provided key evidence for dark matter through galaxy rotation curves.

Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar (1910–1995, India/USA)

Discovered the Chandrasekhar limit for white dwarf stability; Nobel Prize winner.

Cecilia Payne-Gaposchkin (1900–1979, UK/USA)

Determined stars are mostly hydrogen and helium; first to correctly interpret stellar spectra.

Tycho Brahe (1546–1601, Denmark)

Compiled the most accurate pre-telescope astronomical observations.

Nicolaus Copernicus (1473–1543, Poland)

Revived heliocentric model, shifting Earth from universe center.

Claudius Ptolemy (c. 100–170 AD, Egypt)

Authored the geocentric Almagest, dominant for over 1,400 years.

Caroline Herschel (1750–1848, Germany/UK)

Discovered several comets; first woman paid for scientific work.

Kip Thorne (1940–, USA)

Key in gravitational wave detection (LIGO) and black hole visualization; Nobel Prize.
